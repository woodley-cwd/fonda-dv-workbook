# Domestic Violence Workbook - Realistic Scenarios
**Non-Sugar-Coated, Real-Life Cases**

---

## SCENARIO 1: Healthcare Provider (Emergency Department)

### The Scene
A 28-year-old woman arrives at the ED alone at 11 PM on a Friday. Chief complaint: "I fell down the stairs yesterday." 

Upon examination: bruises on her right arm (defensive wounds), a ligature mark on her neck, bruising on her torso (hand prints), and a healing black eye from "2 weeks ago from another fall."

### What You Observe
- She's wearing a turtleneck in July—unusual
- When you ask about the neck mark, her story changes: "Oh, that's from... my necklace."
- Her vitals show elevated BP (stress response)
- She says her partner is "in the waiting room" and she needs to go soon
- Second visit in 8 months for "accidental injury"
- She avoids eye contact when talking about home

### The Questions She'll Face (Multiple Choice)

**Q1: What's your first clinical instinct?**
A) Call security—potential crime scene  
B) Screen for DV using a validated tool (like HARK) in private, away from her partner ✅  
C) Ask her partner about what happened  
D) Document as "fall" and discharge  

*Feedback if B chosen:* Yes. Isolating her from her partner and using a structured screening tool gives her permission to disclose safely. HARK (Humiliation, Afraid, Raped, Kick) is quick and normalizes asking.

*Feedback if other:* Consider—is this the safest approach for her? What barriers might prevent disclosure?

---

**Q2: She discloses abuse but says she'll be "fine" and wants to leave. What now?**
A) Tell her she MUST leave for her safety  
B) Provide written resources, validate, ask about safety, and document her choice ✅  
C) Call her partner to ask his side of the story  
D) Refer her to therapy  

*Feedback if B:* Correct. She knows her risk best. Your job is to believe, resource, and document. Don't coerce.

*Feedback if A:* This undermines her agency. She may not be ready. Coercion can backfire and put her at higher risk.

---

**Q3: Documentation matters in DV cases. What's essential?**
A) Document exact quotes: "He grabbed my neck" not just "neck injury"  
B) Take photos of injuries with consent, note if she refuses  
C) Note the mechanism she reported vs. what injuries show  
D) All of the above ✅  

*Feedback if D:* Yes. This documentation may be used in court. Be specific, factual, objective.

---

### Her Open-Text Reflection
*"What would you actually say to her in this moment—when she's scared, wants to leave the ED, and her partner is waiting?"*

(No "right answer"—shows how they'd actually respond. Examples of good responses: "I see bruises that concern me. I want you to know this isn't your fault. Here are some resources..." / "Your safety matters. Let's talk privately about what's happening.")

---

### Role-Specific Guidance After Scenario
**As a Healthcare Provider, you're uniquely positioned to:**
- Screen all patients for DV (prevalence is 1 in 4 women)
- Provide medical evidence (documentation) that protects survivors legally
- Connect survivors to services before discharge
- Understand that injury patterns can contradict "accident" explanations

**Key Action Steps:**
1. Use a validated screening tool (HARK, HITS, HITS-Rx)
2. Document in medical record (specific, objective)
3. Provide written DV resources at every visit
4. Know your state's mandatory reporting laws
5. Understand trauma responses (withdrawn, minimizing) aren't disbelief

## SCENARIO 2: Law Enforcement (Domestic Disturbance Call)

### The Scene
Police dispatched to a residential address for "domestic disturbance." You arrive to find:

- A 35-year-old woman with a swollen, bruised face and a bleeding lip
- Her 42-year-old partner is in the kitchen, calm, collected
- They have a 7-year-old child in the living room who saw everything
- The woman is sitting on the couch, withdrawn, not making eye contact
- The partner says: "She was being hysterical. I had to restrain her to keep her from hitting me."

### What You Observe
- Fresh injuries on the woman; no visible injuries on the man
- The woman flinches when the man moves
- When you separate them, the woman says: "It's my fault. I made him angry. I don't want to press charges."
- The child is withdrawn, quiet, watching

### The Questions He'll Face

**Q1: What's your first priority?**
A) Listen to the man's version of events first  
B) Separate them, ensure immediate safety, interview each alone ✅  
C) Tell them to "work it out"  
D) Arrest the woman since she admits "starting it"  

*Feedback if B:* Correct. Separate immediately. Interview each party. Safety first.

---

**Q2: The woman is minimizing/not wanting to press charges. Why does this happen?**
A) She's exaggerating the situation  
B) She's being "dramatic"  
C) She's in immediate danger and terrified of retaliation ✅  
D) She's protecting him because of love/loyalty AND fear  

*Feedback if C or D:* Yes. Trauma responses often look like she's defending him. She may face escalated violence for reporting. Her reluctance is a safety indicator.

---

**Q3: How should you handle the child's presence during this incident?**
A) It's none of your business—family matter  
B) Witness to violence = child abuse; separate and interview appropriately ✅  
C) Let the abuser stay with the child  
D) Don't involve child services  

*Feedback if B:* Correct. Witnessing DV is trauma for the child. Document it. May need CPS involvement.

---

**Q4: When documenting, what's critical?**
A) Direct quotes from both parties  
B) Injuries observed (photos if possible)  
C) Behavioral observations (fear, withdrawal, minimizing)  
D) Child's presence and statements  
E) All of the above ✅  

*Feedback if E:* Yes. This creates a record for prosecution later, even if she doesn't cooperate now.

---

### His Open-Text Reflection
*"You're writing the report. What gets documented, and what's the tone you use to ensure this case can be prosecuted later if she does press charges?"*

(Shows if they understand documentation's role in DV cases)

---

### Role-Specific Guidance After Scenario
**As Law Enforcement, you're uniquely positioned to:**
- Be the first responder who sets safety
- Document evidence (photos, statements) that may be used in prosecution
- Understand that her reluctance to press charges is a trauma response, not her "real" desire
- Recognize that leaving is the most dangerous time—your intervention matters

**Key Action Steps:**
1. Always separate parties and interview privately
2. Never minimize ("work it out at home")
3. Document injuries, statements, scene, child presence
4. Take photos of injuries and scene
5. Provide written resources and safety planning info before you leave

## SCENARIO 3: Social Worker / Caseworker (Child Welfare Investigation)

### The Scene
You're a child welfare caseworker. A teacher reports a 6-year-old student who:
- Drew a picture of "Mommy and Daddy fighting"
- Said "Daddy hit Mommy and they yell really loud"
- Has behavioral changes (clingy, anxious, acting out)

You go to the home for an unannounced visit. What you find:

- A 32-year-old mother, overwhelmed, looks exhausted
- A 36-year-old father, charming, minimizes everything
- The 6-year-old is withdrawn, clings to mom
- Mom minimizes: "We argue sometimes, that's normal. He's a good dad, really."
- Dad contradicts her: "We barely fight. She's exaggerating."
- When you ask about what the child witnessed, mom gets nervous, says "It's nothing the child should worry about"

### What You Observe
- Mom has old bruises on her arm (she says she's "clumsy")
- The father controls the conversation
- The mother looks afraid, keeps checking his reaction
- The child draws during your visit—more violent imagery
- No safety plan exists
- Mom has no family support locally
- She works part-time; he manages finances

### The Questions She'll Face

**Q1: The mother says "We're fine, it's just arguments." What's your assessment?**
A) Believe her—document as "no indicators of abuse"  
B) The child witnessed conflict, mother has bruises, and she's minimizing = red flags ✅  
C) The father seems nice, so probably overblown  
D) This is a parental relationship issue, not a DV case  

*Feedback if B:* Correct. Children witnessing DV = emotional abuse and safety concern. Her minimizing + bruises = indicators. This child needs protection.

---

**Q2: The father is controlling the narrative. What's your next move?**
A) Interview them together  
B) Accept his version since he's "calm"  
C) Schedule private, individual interviews—alone—with each parent ✅  
D) Tell them you'll be back when the father isn't home (telegraphing your intent)  

*Feedback if C:* Correct. Never interview together in DV cases. She can't speak freely with him present. Schedule separately to maintain safety.

---

**Q3: The mother admits she's "scared of his reactions" but won't name abuse. What's your role?**
A) Force her to file a police report  
B) Remove the child immediately  
C) Respect her pace, provide resources, build trust, and create a safety plan at her pace ✅  
D) Tell her to leave him  

*Feedback if C:* Yes. Forcing action backfires. She knows her risk. Your job: offer options, provide resources, help her plan for her and her child's safety.

---

**Q4: The mother has no job skills, no money, no family support. What's a realistic barrier to leaving?**
A) She loves him too much  
B) She can't afford childcare, housing, or living expenses without him ✅  
C) She's just being dramatic  
D) She should "just leave"  

*Feedback if B:* Correct. Economic dependence is one of the top barriers. Case planning must address: housing, income, childcare, benefits she's eligible for.

---

### Her Open-Text Reflection
*"You're planning a safety strategy with this mother and child. What's your first conversation with her, and what resources do you prioritize?"*

(Shows if they understand trauma-informed, survival-focused case management)

---

### Role-Specific Guidance After Scenario
**As a Social Services Professional, you're uniquely positioned to:**
- Understand the system landscape (housing, benefits, childcare, employment)
- Build long-term safety plans (not just crisis intervention)
- Center the survivor's agency and timeline
- Address root causes (financial dependence, isolation, trauma)
- Protect the child AND support the non-abusive parent

**Key Action Steps:**
1. Assess safety without him present
2. Don't force leaving—work at her pace
3. Provide concrete resources (housing, benefits, childcare, legal aid)
4. Help her build a safety plan
5. Document and coordinate with law enforcement, schools, healthcare
6. Understand that case removal may be last resort—support the parent to be safe is first

## SCENARIO 4: Faith Leader / Clergy (Pastoral Counseling)

### The Scene
A 38-year-old parishioner, who you've known for 10 years, comes to you in tears. She's part of a tight-knit church community. She says:

"My marriage is struggling. My husband and I argue a lot. I'm trying to be a better wife, but I can't seem to do anything right. I pray about it constantly, and I'm worried I'm failing God by thinking about leaving."

### What You Observe
- She's wearing sunglasses inside (hiding bruises?)
- She mentions her husband "manages her" and "doesn't like her talking to people"
- She's isolated from church friends lately (was very involved before)
- She's full of shame: "I must be doing something wrong."
- She's asking: "What should I do to make him happy?"
- When you ask about "arguments," she gets vague and changes subject
- She keeps saying: "He's a good man. I don't want to leave. God hates divorce."

### The Questions He'll Face

**Q1: What's your first pastoral instinct?**
A) Tell her to pray harder and submit to her husband (biblical authority)  
B) Refer her to marriage counseling to "fix the relationship"  
C) Ask directly about safety; if abuse is present, that's NOT a couples counseling issue ✅  
D) Involve her in more church activities so she's "less focused on problems"  

*Feedback if C:* Correct. DV is NOT a relationship problem to solve together. It's a safety and power issue. If abuse is present, couples counseling is dangerous.

---

**Q2: She says "The Bible says wives should submit." How do you respond?**
A) Affirm that biblical submission is her calling  
B) Tell her that abuse is not what God intends; her safety is spiritual too ✅  
C) Suggest she pray for patience  
D) Avoid the theology—don't take sides  

*Feedback if B:* Correct. Faith communities sometimes use theology to justify abuse. Reframe: God doesn't want her harmed. Her safety IS spiritual.

---

**Q3: She's terrified of divorce ("God hates divorce," shame from church). What's your role?**
A) Reinforce that staying is godly  
B) Affirm her faith AND help her see that abuse is NOT God's will; separation ≠ divorce; safety matters ✅  
C) Refer her to a secular counselor (faith isn't relevant here)  
D) Tell her to forgive and reconcile  

*Feedback if B:* Correct. She needs spiritual permission to prioritize safety. "God doesn't call you to suffer abuse" can be powerful coming from clergy.

---

**Q4: She's isolated and trusts you. What's your responsibility?**
A) Counsel her privately on marriage issues  
B) Connect her to professional DV advocates while offering spiritual support ✅  
C) Talk to her husband to "understand his side"  
D) Tell her to confide in the church community  

*Feedback if B:* Correct. Your role isn't marriage counseling in abuse cases—it's to provide spiritual support AND connect her to professionals.

---

### Her Open-Text Reflection
*"She's crying, ashamed, and terrified. What do you actually say to her in that moment that honors her faith AND empowers her to prioritize her safety?"*

(Shows spiritual sensitivity + DV awareness)

---

### Role-Specific Guidance After Scenario
**As a Faith Leader, you're uniquely positioned to:**
- Provide spiritual permission/reframing ("God wants your safety, not your suffering")
- Offer sanctuary (literal and spiritual) if needed
- Understand faith-based shame and use theology to heal, not harm
- Connect parishioners to professional help while offering pastoral care
- Model that faith communities protect vulnerable people

**Key Action Steps:**
1. Ask directly about safety in pastoral counseling
2. Provide spiritual permission to prioritize safety over "biblical submission"
3. Know DV resources and refer proactively
4. Never do couples counseling if abuse is present
5. Be aware that abusers sometimes use faith/theology as control
6. Understand that she may need time and spiritual support to leave

---

## Summary: What Makes These Scenarios Realistic

✅ **Healthcare:** Injury patterns don't match stories, isolation in waiting room, multiple visits, defensive wounds  
✅ **Law Enforcement:** Victim doesn't want to press charges, child witness, abuser's charming demeanor, documentation challenges  
✅ **Social Worker:** Economic dependence, parental control, child behavioral changes, mother's minimizing, no safety plan  
✅ **Faith Leader:** Theology used as control, shame, isolation from community, shame about divorce, parishioner in trusted relationship

None of these are "simple" or "cleaned up." These are the messy, complex situations professionals actually face.
